Section 1Introduction

Before any team writes a single line of code, before anyone draws a single flowchart, there is one decision that quietly shapes everything that follows — the choice of a process model. It determines how requirements are gathered, how risks are faced, how developers iterate, how stakeholders give feedback, and ultimately whether the final product looks anything like what was originally imagined.

This assignment explores that foundational decision for a specific project: a University Timetable Optimization and Management System. The system is intended to automate the preparation, review, and publication of academic schedules across multiple departments, teachers, student groups, and physical rooms — eliminating the manual, error-prone, spreadsheet-driven timetabling that most universities still endure today.

The goal of this report is not simply to pick a model and move on. The goal is to examine the nature of the project carefully, understand what each candidate process model offers and what it costs, and then make a reasoned, justified choice that any reader — whether a professor or a future developer on the team — could look at and say, "Yes, that makes sense for this kind of project."

A process model is not a bureaucratic formality. It is the architecture of how a team thinks and works. Choosing the wrong one can doom a project as surely as a wrong algorithm or a broken database schema.

Section 2Project Overview

The University Timetable Optimization and Management System is a web-based application built for academic institutions. At its core, the system must do two distinct things that are harder to combine than they might appear at first glance: manage master data, and generate feasible schedules from that data.

On the management side, the system must handle records for departments, semesters, courses, sections, student groups, teachers, rooms, and time slots. Administrators need to configure teacher availability, room capacities, blocked days, holidays, and institutional policy preferences. All of this is fairly standard information management work.

On the optimization side, the system must take that data and produce timetables that avoid direct scheduling conflicts — two teachers in the same room, a section scheduled in two places at once, a teacher assigned during their declared unavailability — while simultaneously trying to satisfy softer preferences like compact student schedules, morning-heavy timetabling during summer terms, and spatial proximity for rooms used in consecutive sessions.

The expected users of the system span five roles: the Timetable Administrator who controls the entire process, the Department Coordinator who oversees academic unit requirements, the Teacher who submits availability and views their own schedule, the Student who accesses published timetables, and the Facility Manager who tracks room usage. Each of these roles interacts with the system differently and has different expectations of what "done" looks like.

This combination — non-trivial data management, algorithmic scheduling, multi-role interaction, and evolving institutional policies — makes the project genuinely complex. It is not a homework CRUD app. It demands a development process mature enough to handle that complexity without collapsing under its own weight.

Section 3Requirements Characterization

To choose a process model rationally, it is necessary to first understand what kind of requirements this project carries. Not all software projects are alike. The nature of the requirements — how stable they are, how well-understood they are, how visual they need to be — has a direct bearing on which process model will serve the team best.

3.1 — What Is Known from the Start

Certain things about the timetable system are clear before development begins. Every academic institution knows its basic entities: teachers have names and availability, rooms have capacities, courses meet a certain number of times per week, and schedules must not produce clashes. These hard constraints can be specified early and with confidence.

3.2 — What Tends to Emerge Later

But there is another category of requirement that shows up in nearly every timetable project: the preferences and edge cases that nobody mentions until they see a draft schedule and react to it. A department head might say, "We did not realize we wanted to specify that third-year courses should be scheduled before noon." A teacher might submit a change request that reveals a missing rule in the system. A room might turn out to be unsuitable for large lectures for reasons that were never documented.

This pattern — early clarity on structure, late discovery of nuanced preferences — is almost universal in scheduling software. Any honest process model selection must account for it.

3.3 — Visual Validation Is Essential

Timetable systems are inherently visual products. A schedule is not meaningful as a database table — it becomes meaningful when it is laid out in a grid and a human being can look at it and say, "This looks reasonable" or "This makes no sense." That means early user feedback on screen design, workflow layout, and schedule presentation is genuinely important, not just a nice-to-have.

3.4 — The Project Has Natural Phases

The system can be broken into logical layers: first the master data, then the rule configuration, then the generation engine, then the review-and-repair workflow, then publication and reporting. These layers depend on each other in a clear sequence. That structural decomposition suggests that phased development — building and validating one layer before investing in the next — is a natural fit.

Section 4Candidate Process Models

Four well-established software development process models were considered as candidates for this project. Each is described below, along with its characteristic strengths and the circumstances where it tends to work well or poorly. A diagram of each model is provided to illustrate how it structures the development lifecycle.

4.1 — The Waterfall Model

The Waterfall Model is the classic sequential development lifecycle, first formalized by Royce in 1970 and later popularized as the canonical "structured" approach to software engineering. It divides development into distinct, non-overlapping phases: Requirements, System Design, Implementation, Testing, Deployment, and Maintenance. Each phase must be fully completed and signed off before the next one begins. The idea is that a project, like water flowing downhill, moves in only one direction.

Waterfall is most powerful when requirements are exceptionally stable and well-understood at the outset — think embedded systems with hardware constraints, or projects with regulatory sign-off requirements at each phase boundary. Its documentation-heavy approach also provides a clear audit trail. Where it struggles is precisely where most real-world software ends up: when user needs evolve, when stakeholders change their minds after seeing a working prototype, or when the project domain contains hidden complexity that only surfaces during implementation.

Figure 1 — The Waterfall Model: sequential, phase-locked, one-way flow

4.2 — The Incremental Model

The Incremental Model decomposes development into a series of smaller, self-contained releases, each of which adds a defined chunk of functionality to what was built before. Rather than trying to deliver the entire system at once, the team makes a series of deliveries — each one working software that can be reviewed, tested, and built upon.

Each increment follows a mini-lifecycle of its own: analysis, design, code, and test, applied to that increment's scope. The system grows progressively, and stakeholders can interact with working versions of the product throughout development, not just at the end. This early and continuous feedback loop is one of the model's most valuable properties.

Figure 2 — The Incremental Model: structured phases, repeated for each delivery

4.3 — The Spiral Model

Proposed by Barry Boehm in 1986, the Spiral Model is a risk-driven development lifecycle that organizes the project around repeated cycles — or spirals — of planning, risk assessment, engineering, and evaluation. It does not separate these activities into distinct sequential phases. Instead, it revisits all of them in every cycle, with the project growing more concrete and the risks becoming smaller with each rotation outward from the center.

The model is particularly well-suited to large, high-stakes projects where technical uncertainty is significant. Its emphasis on risk identification at every stage means that dangerous assumptions get surfaced and dealt with early rather than discovered during integration testing. For a university project on a semester timeline, however, its procedural overhead can outweigh its benefits unless the project genuinely has the kind of critical risk profile that justifies full spiral discipline.

Figure 3 — The Spiral Model (Boehm, 1986): risk-driven, outward-growing cycles

4.4 — Agile / XP Practices

Agile software development is not a single model but a family of methodologies that share a common philosophy: deliver working software early and often, respond to changing requirements rather than fighting them, and prioritize human collaboration over comprehensive documentation. Extreme Programming (XP) is one specific Agile method that emphasizes practices like pair programming, test-driven development, continuous integration, and very short release cycles.

Agile methods are extraordinary in environments where requirements are highly volatile and constant user interaction is possible. They can produce remarkable results. However, they require tight stakeholder collaboration that is not always available in academic project contexts. They can also feel underdisciplined when a course assignment demands formal documented outputs. This does not mean Agile techniques are useless here — it means they work better as tactical practices inside a more formally structured process than as the primary lifecycle model.

Section 5Selection Criteria

Choosing between candidate models requires more than intuition. The following criteria were defined based on the characteristics of this specific project. Each criterion describes a dimension along which the project's needs can be assessed and compared against what each model provides.

  • Requirement volatility. How likely are the requirements to change or grow after development begins? A project with stable, frozen requirements can afford a sequential model. One where requirements are expected to evolve needs a model that accommodates change without treating it as a failure.
  • Project complexity. Does the system do more than store and retrieve data? Does it include algorithmic behavior, constraint processing, rule management, or other non-trivial logic? Higher complexity favors staged development.
  • Need for visual validation. Do stakeholders need to see working screens and timetable layouts early in the process, rather than just reading requirement documents? Early visual feedback reduces the risk of building the wrong interface.
  • Technical risk. Are there areas of the project where the right technical approach is uncertain? The scheduling engine involves decisions about constraint handling and optimization that carry real implementation risk.
  • Documentation requirements. This is an academic project. It must produce not just working software but also formal documentation at each stage. Some models are more documentation-friendly than others.
  • Phased deliverability. Can the project be broken into meaningful, independently testable pieces? A model that supports phased delivery reduces the all-or-nothing risk of a single end-of-semester submission.
  • Testing feasibility. Can different parts of the system be tested independently, or must everything come together before any meaningful testing is possible?

Section 7Selected Process Model

The Incremental Process Model has been selected as the primary development lifecycle for this project. This is not a default choice — it is the result of comparing the specific characteristics of a university timetable system against what each model offers.

One important distinction must be stated clearly: prototyping is not selected as a co-equal lifecycle model. Instead, prototype-quality screens and workflows will be built during early increments specifically to validate user interface decisions and surface hidden requirements. Once that validation is complete, development proceeds on firm incremental footing. Prototyping is a technique used within the lifecycle, not the lifecycle itself.

Section 8Justification of the Selected Model

8.1 — Requirements Are Practically Guaranteed to Evolve

There is a well-known pattern in timetable software development: the first time a stakeholder sees a generated schedule, they discover requirements they did not know they had. Maybe it is a preference for keeping consecutive classes in nearby rooms. Maybe it is a department rule about not scheduling core courses on Fridays. Maybe a teacher submits a change request that reveals a gap in how the system handles exception cases. In this kind of project, requirement evolution is not a failure of analysis — it is a structural property of the domain. Any process model that treats late requirement changes as exceptional and costly is the wrong model here.

8.2 — The System Has Genuine Depth

It would be a mistake to think of this project as a straightforward web application. The scheduling part — generating conflict-free timetables while satisfying soft preferences — is a constrained optimization problem with non-trivial behavior. Rule interactions can produce unexpected outcomes. Score functions behave differently across different data configurations. The system can produce technically valid schedules that nonetheless feel wrong to the people who have to use them. This kind of depth is best approached layer by layer, not all at once.

8.3 — Visual Feedback Is Not Optional

The single most important user-facing output of this system is a visual timetable grid. The difference between a good timetable interface and a bad one is something users can recognize immediately but may struggle to articulate upfront. Early prototypes of the schedule view, the conflict inspection panel, and the change-request workflow are not luxury features — they are how the team learns what the system actually needs to feel like to the people using it. The incremental model accommodates this by making early feedback an expected part of the process rather than a deviation from the plan.

8.4 — The Project Decomposes Naturally

One of the strongest arguments for an incremental approach is that this particular project falls apart into a natural sequence of layers. You genuinely cannot build the scheduling engine before you have the master data. You genuinely cannot build the review workflow before you have something to review. The dependency chain between components is not arbitrary — it reflects the domain's logic. This means the incremental model is not just philosophically appropriate; it is structurally aligned with how the project actually needs to be built.

8.5 — Testing Becomes Manageable

When a project is built incrementally, testing can be applied to each delivery as it is completed. Data validation rules can be tested before the scheduling engine exists. The scheduling engine can be tested before the publication workflow is built. This staged testing approach means that by the time the system is complete, most of it has already been tested in isolation. The alternative — testing everything at the end — creates an enormous integration risk that is particularly dangerous given an academic semester deadline.

8.6 — It Supports Future Algorithmic Refinement

The selection of the core scheduling algorithm — whether constraint propagation, simulated annealing, or a hybrid approach — is a decision that benefits from having the surrounding system already working. When the web application, data model, and rule-configuration workflow are in place, the team can experiment with different generation strategies without destabilizing the rest of the system. An incremental model makes this possible; a Waterfall model would force that decision far too early.

Section 9Why Other Models Were Not Selected

9.1 — Waterfall: The Wrong Shape for This Problem

Waterfall assumes that requirements can be completely and correctly captured before design begins, that design can be completed before implementation begins, and that implementation can be finished before testing begins. In a timetable system where stakeholder preferences surface gradually through iterative draft reviews, that assumption is simply untrue. The cost of late changes in a Waterfall process is high — late-stage requirement changes require backing up through phases that are supposed to be complete. That is not a price worth paying for a project where late discovery of requirements is not an accident but a feature of the domain.

9.2 — Spiral: Powerful but Disproportionate

The Spiral Model would be genuinely excellent for a large, mission-critical timetabling system being deployed across a national university network with significant regulatory requirements. Its risk-driven structure would be invaluable. But for a semester-scale academic project, the procedural overhead of full spiral discipline — formal risk assessments at each cycle, prototyping plans, stakeholder sign-offs at every turn — creates more process than the project needs. The timetable system has real risks, but they are manageable through good incremental planning without the full weight of a spiral framework.

9.3 — Agile/XP: Good Tactics, Wrong Primary Frame

Agile principles are attractive, and there is no reason the team cannot use Agile-adjacent practices during development — short planning cycles, iterative builds, frequent testing. But Agile as a primary process model presupposes a level of continuous stakeholder availability and adaptability that is harder to guarantee in an academic context. It also tends to deprioritize formal documentation in favor of working software, which is the right trade-off in a commercial startup but not in a course assignment that expects documented deliverables at each stage. Agile practices will inform the implementation style; they will not govern the formal process model.

Section 10Proposed Increment Plan

The following roadmap translates the selected model into a concrete sequence of deliveries. Each increment is designed to be independently reviewable, buildable, and testable. The sequence respects the natural dependency structure of the system while ensuring that something meaningful is working and demonstrable at each stage.

  • 01
    Academic Structure & Resource Setup

    Departments, semesters, courses, sections, teachers, rooms, and time slots. This is the foundational data layer that everything else depends on. By the end of this increment, the system has its entire entity model working with proper validations, relationships, and basic admin screens.

  • 02
    Constraint & Policy Configuration

    Teacher availability windows, room suitability flags, blocked days, holidays, and administrative preference settings. Early prototyping of these configuration screens also serves as the primary visual validation phase — stakeholder feedback here will reveal most of the hidden preferences before the scheduling engine is built.

  • 03
    Core Timetable Generation (MVP)

    The initial scheduling engine that produces a conflict-free timetable from the configured data. This increment introduces hard-constraint validation and a basic conflict view so that generated schedules can be inspected. The generation algorithm does not need to be optimal at this stage — it needs to be correct and reviewable.

  • 04
    Review, Repair & Controlled Re-optimization

    Draft timetable inspection, manual change request handling, conflict explanation panels, and limited re-optimization after approved changes. This is the increment where the system transitions from "generator" to "management tool" — the difference between a one-shot scheduler and an ongoing operational system.

  • 05
    Publication, Filtered Views & Operational Reports

    Final timetable publishing, role-based views for teachers and students, room-wise utilization reports, export functionality, and holiday-aware display. This increment completes the user-facing product and makes the system genuinely useful to all five roles in their daily work.

Section 12Conclusion

The process of selecting a development lifecycle model is, at its core, an act of matching. It is about looking honestly at what a project actually is — what it needs, where it will fail, what it does not yet know about itself — and finding a structure that fits those realities rather than forcing the project into a shape it does not naturally have.

For the University Timetable Optimization and Management System, the Incremental Process Model is that fit. The project will encounter requirement evolution — it is that kind of domain. The project has natural structural layers that benefit from sequential delivery. The project's users need to see working interfaces early. The project carries real technical risk in its scheduling engine that is better mitigated through staged development than through a single high-stakes integration at the end.

The Waterfall model would freeze requirements that cannot be frozen. The Spiral model would impose procedural overhead that the project does not need at its scale. Agile practices are valuable but are better positioned as implementation-level tactics than as the primary process framework for an academic assignment requiring structured deliverables.

The Incremental Model gives this project the flexibility to learn from its own development without losing the structure that makes that development documentable, testable, and academically defensible. That is the right combination for a project of this kind, at this scale, on this timeline.

Prototyping will be used in early increments to validate screens and surface hidden preferences, but it will not govern the lifecycle. Development will proceed one meaningful, reviewable layer at a time. The final result will be a system that was not just built, but grown — each increment making the next one clearer, more confident, and more correct.
